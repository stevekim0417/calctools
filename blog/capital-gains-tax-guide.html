What Are Capital Gains?

A capital gain occurs when you sell a "capital asset" for more than its original purchase price (called the "cost basis"). Capital assets include:

  • Investments: Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs
  • Real estate: Homes, rental properties, land
  • Personal property: Cars, jewelry, collectibles, art
  • Cryptocurrency: Bitcoin, Ethereum, other digital assets
  • Business assets: Equipment, intellectual property

üìä The Basic Formula

Capital Gain = Sale Price ‚àí Cost Basis ‚àí Selling Expenses

If the result is negative, you have a capital loss, which can offset other gains or reduce taxable income.

Example

You bought 100 shares of ABC stock for $50/share ($5,000 total) and sold them for $80/share ($8,000 total), paying $10 in commissions.

  • Sale price: $8,000
  • Cost basis: $5,000
  • Expenses: $10
  • Capital gain: $2,990

Short-Term vs. Long-Term Capital Gains

The tax rate on your capital gain depends on how long you held the asset before selling it:

Holding PeriodClassificationTax Rate
1 year or lessShort-TermOrdinary income rates (10%-37%)
More than 1 yearLong-TermPreferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%)
üí° Key Insight: The difference can be huge. A taxpayer in the 32% bracket pays only 15% on long-term gains ‚Äî a savings of 17 percentage points. On a $50,000 gain, that's $8,500 in tax savings just by holding an extra day!

How the Holding Period Works

  • The clock starts the day after you purchase
  • The clock stops the day you sell
  • You need to hold for at least one year and one day for long-term treatment

Example: If you buy stock on January 15, 2026, you must wait until at least January 16, 2027 to sell for long-term capital gains treatment.

  • Early retirees before Social Security begins
  • Students or those with gap years
  • Strategic "tax gain harvesting" to reset cost basis

How to Calculate Capital Gains Tax

Step 1: Determine Your Cost Basis

Cost basis is typically what you paid, plus certain adjustments:

  • Purchase price of the asset
  • Transaction costs: Commissions, fees
  • Improvements: For real estate, capital improvements increase basis
  • Reinvested dividends: If dividends were reinvested, add them to basis

Step 2: Calculate Your Gain or Loss

Gain/Loss = Net Proceeds ‚àí Adjusted Cost Basis

Step 3: Categorize as Short-Term or Long-Term

Check how long you held the asset (more than 1 year = long-term).

Step 4: Net Your Gains and Losses

  • Short-term gains and losses are netted together
  • Long-term gains and losses are netted together
  • If one category has a net loss, it offsets gains in the other category

Step 5: Apply the Appropriate Rate

Apply short-term rates to net short-term gains and long-term rates to net long-term gains.

Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)

High earners face an additional 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax on top of regular capital gains taxes.

Who Pays NIIT?

NIIT applies when your Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) exceeds:

  • Single: $200,000
  • Married Filing Jointly: $250,000
  • Head of Household: $200,000

What Income Is Subject to NIIT?

  • Capital gains
  • Dividends
  • Interest (excluding municipal bonds)
  • Rental income
  • Royalties
  • Passive business income

‚ö†Ô∏è Total Tax on Long-Term Gains for High Earners

If you're subject to NIIT and in the 20% long-term bracket, your effective rate is:

20% + 3.8% = 23.8%

Still much better than the 37% top ordinary income rate!

Special Situations

Primary Residence Exclusion

When you sell your primary home, you can exclude significant gains from taxes:

  • Single: Exclude up to $250,000 in gains
  • Married Filing Jointly: Exclude up to $500,000 in gains

Requirements:

  • Owned the home for at least 2 of the last 5 years
  • Lived in it as your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years
  • Haven't used this exclusion in the past 2 years

Inherited Assets: Step-Up in Basis

When you inherit an asset, your cost basis "steps up" to the fair market value at the time of death. This can eliminate years of appreciation from capital gains.

üí° Example: Your grandmother bought stock for $10,000 in 1980, worth $200,000 when she passed. Your cost basis is $200,000, not $10,000. If you sell for $205,000, you only pay tax on $5,000 in gains ‚Äî not $195,000.

Collectibles and Precious Metals

Long-term gains on collectibles (art, antiques, coins, etc.) are taxed at a maximum rate of 28%, not the standard 0/15/20% rates.

Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS)

Gains from qualified small business stock held more than 5 years may be 100% excluded from federal tax (up to $10 million or 10x your basis).

Tax-Saving Strategies

1. Hold for Long-Term Treatment

The simplest strategy: hold investments for more than one year to qualify for lower long-term rates.

2. Tax-Loss Harvesting

Sell losing investments to offset gains:

  • Losses first offset gains of the same type (short-term vs. long-term)
  • Excess losses offset the other type
  • Up to $3,000 of net losses can offset ordinary income annually
  • Remaining losses carry forward indefinitely

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch the Wash Sale Rule

You can't claim a loss if you buy a "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale. The IRS disallows the loss in this case.

3. Tax Gain Harvesting

If you're in a low-income year (retired early, job gap, etc.), you may be in the 0% long-term bracket. Strategically selling winners to "harvest gains" at 0% resets your cost basis higher.

4. Hold Investments in Tax-Advantaged Accounts

  • 401(k), IRA: Gains grow tax-deferred (or tax-free for Roth)
  • HSA: Gains are tax-free if used for medical expenses

5. Asset Location Strategy

Place investments strategically:

  • Tax-advantaged accounts: High-yield bonds, REITs (taxed as ordinary income)
  • Taxable accounts: Growth stocks, index funds (tax-efficient, lower turnover)

6. Donate Appreciated Assets

Donating appreciated stock to charity lets you deduct the full market value without paying capital gains tax on the appreciation.

7. Qualified Opportunity Zones

Reinvesting gains into Qualified Opportunity Zone funds can defer and potentially reduce capital gains taxes.

Capital Losses: Turning Lemons into Lemonade

Capital losses aren't just bad news ‚Äî they're a tax planning tool:

How Losses Work

  1. Short-term losses offset short-term gains first
  2. Long-term losses offset long-term gains first
  3. Net losses from one category offset gains in the other
  4. Up to $3,000 of net losses offset ordinary income ($1,500 if married filing separately)
  5. Excess losses carry forward to future years
üí° Strategy: If you have both gains and losses, consider whether it's better to offset short-term gains (taxed at higher ordinary rates) or let them offset long-term gains. Sometimes harvesting short-term losses specifically is more valuable.

Cryptocurrency and Capital Gains

The IRS treats cryptocurrency as property, not currency. This means:

Taxable Events

  • Selling crypto for cash: Capital gain/loss
  • Trading one crypto for another: Capital gain/loss
  • Using crypto to buy goods/services: Capital gain/loss
  • Receiving crypto as payment: Ordinary income + future capital gain/loss

Non-Taxable Events

  • Buying crypto with cash
  • Transferring between your own wallets
  • Gifting (though recipient may owe later)

Cost Basis Methods

For crypto, you can typically use:

  • FIFO (First In, First Out): Sell oldest coins first
  • Specific Identification: Choose which coins to sell

‚ö†Ô∏è Crypto Reporting

Starting in 2026, cryptocurrency exchanges must report transactions to the IRS via Form 1099-DA. Keep detailed records of all your transactions!

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I owe capital gains on investments I haven't sold?

No. Capital gains tax is only triggered when you sell. Unrealized gains (paper profits) are not taxed until you realize them by selling.

What if I sell at a loss?

Losses can offset gains and up to $3,000 can offset ordinary income. Excess losses carry forward to future years.

Are dividends taxed as capital gains?

Qualified dividends receive the same preferential rates as long-term capital gains. Ordinary dividends are taxed as ordinary income.

How do I report capital gains?

Report capital gains and losses on Schedule D of Form 1040, with details on Form 8949. Your broker provides Form 1099-B with transaction details.

Can I avoid capital gains by reinvesting?

Generally no ‚Äî selling triggers the tax even if you immediately reinvest. Exception: 1031 exchanges for real estate and Opportunity Zone investments can defer gains.

What about state capital gains taxes?

Most states tax capital gains as ordinary income. Some states (like Washington) have separate capital gains taxes. A few states (like Florida and Texas) have no income tax at all.

Summary

Capital gains taxes are a crucial consideration for any investor. Key takeaways:

  • Holding period matters: Long-term gains (>1 year) are taxed at preferential 0%, 15%, or 20% rates
  • Short-term gains are taxed at your ordinary income rate (up to 37%)
  • High earners may owe an additional 3.8% NIIT
  • Tax-loss harvesting can offset gains and reduce taxes
  • Special rules apply to home sales, inheritances, and collectibles

Smart tax planning throughout the year ‚Äî not just at tax time ‚Äî can significantly reduce your capital gains tax burden while keeping your investment strategy on track.
