What Are Payroll Taxes?

Payroll taxes are taxes deducted from your wages by your employer and remitted to the government. They fund specific programs and are separate from federal income tax.

üìä Types of Payroll Taxes

  • FICA Taxes: Social Security and Medicare (you + employer)
  • Federal Income Tax: Withheld based on your W-4
  • State Income Tax: In most states
  • Local Taxes: City/county taxes in some areas
  • FUTA/SUTA: Unemployment taxes (employer-paid)

FICA Taxes: Social Security & Medicare

FICA stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act. These taxes fund two major programs:

Social Security Tax

Social Security provides retirement, disability, and survivor benefits to eligible Americans.

  • Employee rate: 6.2%
  • Employer rate: 6.2%
  • Total: 12.4%
  • 2026 Wage Base: $176,100 (tax only applies to wages up to this amount)

Medicare Tax

Medicare provides health insurance for people 65 and older, plus some younger people with disabilities.

  • Employee rate: 1.45%
  • Employer rate: 1.45%
  • Total: 2.9%
  • Wage Base: No limit (applies to all wages)

Additional Medicare Tax

High earners pay an extra Medicare surtax:

  • Rate: 0.9% (employee only ‚Äî employer doesn't match)
  • Threshold: Wages over $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly)
üí° Combined FICA Rate: As an employee, you pay 7.65% of your wages in FICA taxes (6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare). Your employer pays another 7.65%, for a total of 15.3% going to these programs.

What Happens at the Social Security Wage Cap?

Once your wages reach $176,100, Social Security tax stops being withheld for the rest of the year. You'll see a bump in your take-home pay!

üìà High Earner Example

Salary: $250,000 | Paid bi-weekly ($9,615.38/paycheck)

  • Jan-Aug: Full FICA withheld ($735.58/check)
  • Sept (after ~$176K): Social Security stops; only Medicare ($139.42/check)
  • After $200K: Additional Medicare kicks in (+0.9%)

Result: Bigger paychecks in the second half of the year!

Federal Income Tax Withholding

Unlike FICA (which is a fixed percentage), federal income tax withholding depends on:

  • Your filing status (single, married, head of household)
  • Number of dependents
  • Additional withholding you request
  • Pre-tax deductions (401k, HSA, etc.)

How Withholding Is Calculated

Your employer uses the information on your Form W-4, along with IRS withholding tables, to estimate your annual tax liability and withhold accordingly each paycheck.

The Goal: Match Your Actual Tax Liability

Ideally, your total withholding should equal your actual tax owed:

  • Withheld too much? You get a refund (nice, but you gave the government an interest-free loan)
  • Withheld too little? You owe money at tax time (and possibly penalties)
  • Just right? Small refund or small amount owed ‚Äî optimal!

State and Local Payroll Taxes

State Income Tax

Most states impose an income tax that's withheld from your paycheck. Rates vary widely:

Local/City Taxes

Some cities and counties impose their own payroll taxes:

  • New York City: 3.078% - 3.876%
  • Philadelphia: 3.75%
  • Detroit: 2.4%
  • San Francisco: Various payroll taxes on employers

State Disability Insurance (SDI)

A few states require employees to pay for state disability insurance:

  • California: 1.1% (up to $153,164)
  • New York: 0.5% (capped)
  • New Jersey: Variable rates
  • Rhode Island, Hawaii: Also have SDI

Employer-Paid Payroll Taxes

Your employer pays additional payroll taxes that don't appear on your pay stub:

Employer's FICA Share

Your employer matches your 6.2% Social Security and 1.45% Medicare contributions. This is part of the "true cost" of employing you.

Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA)

  • Rate: 6.0% on first $7,000 of wages
  • Credit: Up to 5.4% for state unemployment taxes paid
  • Effective rate: Usually 0.6% (with full credit)
  • Max per employee: $42/year

State Unemployment Tax (SUTA)

  • Rates vary by state and employer's claims history
  • New employers pay a standard rate; experienced employers pay based on claims
  • Wage bases range from $7,000 to $62,500+ depending on state
üí° Your "True" Cost to Employer: If you earn $75,000, your employer actually pays about $81,000+ when including their share of FICA, FUTA, SUTA, workers' comp insurance, and benefits administration.

Understanding Your Pay Stub

A typical pay stub shows these deductions:

Mandatory Deductions (Taxes)

  • Federal Income Tax: Based on your W-4
  • Social Security (OASDI): 6.2% of gross
  • Medicare: 1.45% of gross
  • State Income Tax: Varies by state
  • Local/City Tax: If applicable
  • SDI/SUI: If your state requires

Voluntary Pre-Tax Deductions

  • 401(k)/403(b): Retirement contributions
  • Health Insurance: Your premium share
  • HSA/FSA: Health savings accounts
  • Dental/Vision: If separate from health
  • Transit Benefits: Pre-tax commuter expenses

Post-Tax Deductions

  • Roth 401(k): After-tax retirement
  • Life Insurance: Premiums over $50K coverage
  • Garnishments: Court-ordered (child support, debt)
  • Union Dues: If applicable

Pre-Tax Deductions That Reduce Payroll Taxes

Certain deductions reduce your taxable wages, lowering both income tax AND FICA taxes:

Retirement Accounts

  • Traditional 401(k): Contributions reduce taxable wages (but NOT Social Security/Medicare wages in most cases)
  • 403(b), 457: Similar to 401(k) for nonprofits and government

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Distinction

Traditional 401(k) contributions reduce your federal income tax withholding but usually do NOT reduce your FICA tax base. You still pay Social Security and Medicare on the full amount. HSA contributions, however, reduce BOTH.

Health Savings Account (HSA)

HSA contributions are one of the best tax deals available:

  • Reduces federal income tax
  • Reduces FICA taxes (Social Security + Medicare)
  • Reduces state income tax (in most states)
  • 2026 limits: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family

Flexible Spending Accounts (FSA)

  • Health FSA: Up to $3,200 (2026) ‚Äî reduces income and FICA taxes
  • Dependent Care FSA: Up to $5,000 ‚Äî reduces income and FICA taxes

Pre-Tax Commuter Benefits

  • Transit/Parking: Up to $325/month each (2026)
  • Reduces income tax and FICA taxes

Adjusting Your W-4 for Optimal Withholding

Form W-4 tells your employer how much federal income tax to withhold. Updating it after life changes helps avoid surprises.

When to Update Your W-4

  • Marriage or divorce
  • Birth or adoption of a child
  • New job or second job
  • Major income changes
  • Buying a home (mortgage interest deduction)
  • Large refund or tax bill last year

W-4 Tips

  • Multiple jobs? Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator
  • Want bigger paychecks? Claim more dependents or deductions (risk owing at tax time)
  • Want a bigger refund? Request extra withholding (Step 4c)
  • Self-employed spouse? Request extra withholding to cover their income

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my first paycheck of the year smaller?

It's usually the opposite ‚Äî Social Security tax starts fresh, so you're below the wage cap. However, benefit enrollment changes or tax law updates at year-end can affect January paychecks.

Can I opt out of paying FICA taxes?

Generally, no. FICA is mandatory for most workers. Limited exceptions exist for certain religious groups, some foreign workers, and specific student employment situations.

What if I have multiple jobs?

You might over-pay Social Security if combined wages exceed the wage base. You'll get excess Social Security tax refunded when you file your tax return. Update your W-4s to balance income tax withholding.

Do payroll taxes fund other programs?

FICA specifically funds Social Security and Medicare. Federal income tax (separate from payroll tax) funds general government operations including defense, education, infrastructure, and more.

What's the difference between gross pay and net pay?

Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions. Net pay (take-home pay) is what you actually receive after taxes and other deductions are subtracted.

Will I get back Social Security tax I paid?

Not directly as a refund ‚Äî but you'll receive Social Security benefits when you retire (or if you become disabled). The amount depends on your lifetime earnings and when you claim benefits.

How do I check if my employer is withholding correctly?

Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator at irs.gov. Enter your income, filing status, and current withholding to see if you're on track or need to adjust your W-4.

Summary

Payroll taxes take a significant chunk of your paycheck, but understanding them helps you plan your finances better. Key takeaways:

  • FICA taxes (Social Security + Medicare) total 7.65% of your wages ‚Äî your employer pays another 7.65%
  • Social Security tax (6.2%) only applies to wages up to $176,100 in 2026
  • Medicare tax (1.45%) applies to all wages, with an additional 0.9% on high earners
  • Federal income tax withholding is based on your W-4 and uses progressive rates
  • Pre-tax deductions like HSA and FSA reduce both income and FICA taxes
  • Review your W-4 after major life changes to optimize withholding
