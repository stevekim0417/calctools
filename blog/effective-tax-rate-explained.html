Marginal Tax Rate Explained

üìå Definition

Your marginal tax rate is the rate you pay on your next dollar of income. It's determined by which tax bracket your top dollar of income falls into.

The US has a progressive tax system with seven brackets. As your income increases, portions of your income get taxed at progressively higher rates.

Why it matters: Your marginal rate tells you how much of an extra dollar you'll keep. If you're in the 22% bracket, earning an extra $1,000 means $220 goes to federal taxes and you keep $780.

Effective Tax Rate Explained

üìå Definition

Your effective tax rate (also called average tax rate) is your total tax divided by your total income. It represents what you actually pay overall.

Effective Rate = Total Tax Paid √∑ Total Income √ó 100

Because the US uses marginal brackets, your effective rate is always lower than your marginal rate (unless all your income is in the 10% bracket).

‚ùå Common Myth: "I'll earn less if I move to a higher bracket"

FALSE! Only the income within the new bracket gets taxed at the higher rate. You never take home less money by earning more. A raise always increases your after-tax income.

Why Both Rates Matter

When to Use Marginal Rate

  • Evaluating a raise: How much will you actually keep?
  • Tax-loss harvesting: What's the tax savings from a deduction?
  • Roth vs. Traditional: Deciding on retirement account types
  • Side income: How much will freelance income be taxed?

When to Use Effective Rate

  • Budgeting: What's your actual take-home pay?
  • Comparing tax burdens: Year-over-year or with others
  • Financial planning: Understanding your true tax cost
  • Retirement planning: Estimating future tax liability
