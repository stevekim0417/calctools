Investment Return Basics

Investment returns come from two sources:

  1. Capital appreciation: Your investment increases in value (stock price goes up)
  2. Income: Cash payments you receive (dividends, interest)

Total return combines both. For example, if a stock goes up 5% and pays a 2% dividend, your total return is 7%.

Simple Return Calculation

The basic formula for calculating return:

üìê Return Formula

Return = (Ending Value - Beginning Value + Income) / Beginning Value √ó 100

Example: Bought for $1,000, now worth $1,150, received $30 dividends

Return = ($1,150 - $1,000 + $30) / $1,000 √ó 100 = 18%

Annualized Returns

To compare investments held for different periods, we use annualized returns (also called CAGR‚ÄîCompound Annual Growth Rate):

If $10,000 grows to $16,000 over 5 years:

  • Total return: 60%
  • Annualized return: 9.86% per year

Annualized returns account for compounding and let you compare a 2-year investment with a 10-year one on equal terms.

The Rule of 72

A quick way to estimate how long it takes to double your money:

üí° Rule of 72: Divide 72 by your annual return rate to get the years to double.
  • At 6% return: 72 √∑ 6 = 12 years to double
  • At 8% return: 72 √∑ 8 = 9 years to double
  • At 10% return: 72 √∑ 10 = 7.2 years to double

Why Starting Early Matters So Much

Consider two investors:

  • Early Emma: Invests $5,000/year from age 25-35 (10 years, $50,000 total), then stops
  • Late Larry: Invests $5,000/year from age 35-65 (30 years, $150,000 total)

At 8% returns, by age 65:

  • Emma: ~$787,000 (invested only $50,000)
  • Larry: ~$611,000 (invested $150,000)

Emma invested one-third as much money but ended up with more because of 10 extra years of compounding.

Real vs. Nominal Returns

There's a crucial difference between:

  • Nominal return: The raw percentage gain (what you see reported)
  • Real return: Return after subtracting inflation (your actual purchasing power gain)

üìê Real Return Formula

Real Return ‚âà Nominal Return - Inflation Rate

If stocks return 10% but inflation is 3%, your real return is ~7%

The Risk-Return Relationship

A fundamental principle of investing: higher expected returns require accepting higher risk.

Time Reduces Risk

While stocks are risky in any single year, time smooths out volatility:

  • 1 year: Stocks can lose 30%+ or gain 40%+
  • 10 years: Range narrows to roughly -1% to +18% annually
  • 20 years: Historically, stocks have always been positive

This is why stocks are appropriate for long-term goals (retirement 20+ years away) but not for money you need next year.

Calculating Your Portfolio Returns

Time-Weighted vs. Money-Weighted Returns

When you add or withdraw money throughout the year, calculating returns gets complicated:

  • Time-weighted return (TWR): Measures the investment's performance, ignoring cash flows. Used to compare fund managers.
  • Money-weighted return (MWR/IRR): Measures your personal return, accounting for when you added money. This is what matters for your wealth.

The Impact of Dollar-Cost Averaging

If you invest regularly (like 401k contributions), you naturally buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when high. This can help smooth out the ride, though it doesn't guarantee better returns than lump-sum investing.

üí° Dollar-Cost Averaging Example:

$500/month into a stock that fluctuates:

  • Month 1: $50/share ‚Üí buy 10 shares
  • Month 2: $40/share ‚Üí buy 12.5 shares
  • Month 3: $60/share ‚Üí buy 8.3 shares

Average cost: $48.65/share (better than the $50 average price)

The Sequence of Returns Risk

Average returns hide an important reality: when returns happen matters.

Two scenarios with the same 7% average return:

  • Good sequence: Strong returns early, weak returns later ‚Üí More wealth
  • Bad sequence: Weak returns early, strong returns later ‚Üí Less wealth

This is especially dangerous during retirement withdrawals. A bear market in your first retirement years can devastate a portfolio.

Common Investment Return Myths

Myth 1: "I Need to Beat the Market"

Most professional fund managers fail to beat index funds over the long term. Simply matching the market through low-cost index funds is a winning strategy for most investors.

Myth 2: "Higher Risk Always Means Higher Returns"

Risk increases the range of possible outcomes, not guaranteed returns. Gambling is risky but has negative expected returns. Smart investing means taking compensated risks.

Myth 3: "I Can Time the Market"

Missing just the 10 best days in the market over 20 years can cut your returns in half. Time in the market beats timing the market.

Myth 4: "Last Year's Winner Will Win Again"

Chasing performance is one of the biggest investor mistakes. Today's hot sector often becomes tomorrow's laggard. Diversification beats prediction.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's a good return on investment?

For a diversified portfolio, 7-10% nominal (4-7% real) annually is reasonable for long-term expectations. Any single year could be much higher or lower.

Should I expect the same returns as the historical average?

Many experts believe future returns may be slightly lower than historical averages due to current valuations and lower interest rates. Planning with 6-7% instead of 10% is prudent.

How do fees affect my returns?

A 1% annual fee compounds just like returns do‚Äîbut against you. Over 30 years, 1% in fees can reduce your final portfolio by 25% or more. Choose low-cost index funds (0.03-0.20% expense ratios).

Are dividends reinvested included in return figures?

"Total return" figures include reinvested dividends. "Price return" only shows price changes. Always compare total returns when evaluating investments.
