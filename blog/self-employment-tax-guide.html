What Is Self-Employment Tax?

Self-employment tax is essentially the Social Security and Medicare taxes that self-employed individuals must pay. When you work for an employer, these taxes (called FICA) are split 50/50 between you and your employer. But when you're self-employed, you pay both halves.

üìä Self-Employment Tax Components

  • Social Security: 12.4% on earnings up to $176,100 (2026 wage base)
  • Medicare: 2.9% on all earnings (no cap)
  • Additional Medicare: 0.9% on earnings over $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (married)
  • Total SE Tax: 15.3% (+ 0.9% additional Medicare if applicable)

This is in addition to your regular federal and state income taxes. So a self-employed person can easily face a combined marginal rate of 35-50% or more when including SE tax, federal income tax, and state income tax.

Who Pays Self-Employment Tax?

You owe self-employment tax if you have net self-employment income of $400 or more during the year. This includes:

  • Freelancers and independent contractors (receive 1099-NEC forms)
  • Gig workers: Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart, Fiverr, Upwork, etc.
  • Sole proprietors running their own business
  • Partners in a partnership (their distributive share)
  • Single-member LLC owners (unless elected to be taxed as a corporation)
  • Side hustlers with income from selling goods/services

Who Doesn't Pay SE Tax?

  • W-2 employees (employer pays their share)
  • S-Corp shareholders on their salary portion (but must pay on distributions if salary is unreasonably low)
  • Passive income like rental income (in most cases)
  • Investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains)

How to Calculate Self-Employment Tax

Step 1: Calculate Net Self-Employment Income

Start with your gross self-employment income and subtract all legitimate business expenses:

Net SE Income = Gross Income ‚àí Business Expenses

Step 2: Apply the 92.35% Factor

The IRS allows you to calculate SE tax on only 92.35% of your net earnings (equivalent to the employer-half deduction that W-2 workers get):

SE Tax Base = Net SE Income √ó 0.9235

Step 3: Calculate Social Security Portion

Apply the 12.4% Social Security rate to the lesser of:

  • Your SE Tax Base, OR
  • $176,100 minus any W-2 wages already subject to Social Security

Step 4: Calculate Medicare Portion

Apply the 2.9% Medicare rate to your entire SE Tax Base (no cap).

If applicable, add 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on amounts over the threshold.

Step 5: Add Together

Total SE Tax = Social Security Tax + Medicare Tax + Additional Medicare (if any)

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

Unlike W-2 employees who have taxes withheld automatically, self-employed individuals must pay taxes throughout the year via quarterly estimated payments.

How Much to Pay Quarterly

To avoid underpayment penalties, you must pay the lesser of:

  • 90% of your current year tax liability, OR
  • 100% of your prior year tax liability (110% if AGI was over $150,000)

Safe Harbor Method

The simplest approach: pay 100% (or 110%) of last year's total tax divided by four. This guarantees no underpayment penalty, even if you earn more this year.

üí° Pro Tip: If your income varies significantly, you can use the "annualized income installment method" to pay less in quarters when you earned less. This is more complex but can improve cash flow.

How to Make Payments

  • IRS Direct Pay: irs.gov/payments (free, direct from bank account)
  • EFTPS: Electronic Federal Tax Payment System
  • Credit/Debit Card: Through approved processors (fees apply)
  • Check: Mail Form 1040-ES with payment

Deductions to Reduce Your Tax Burden

One major advantage of self-employment is the ability to deduct legitimate business expenses, which reduces both your income tax AND self-employment tax.

Self-Employment Tax Deduction

You can deduct 50% of your SE tax from your gross income. This is an "above-the-line" deduction that reduces your AGI.

Health Insurance Deduction

Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health insurance premiums for themselves, spouse, and dependents ‚Äî including dental and long-term care. This is an above-the-line deduction.

üè† Home Office Deduction

If you use part of your home regularly and exclusively for business, you can deduct a portion of housing costs. Two methods:

  • Simplified: $5 per square foot, max 300 sq ft = up to $1,500
  • Regular: Calculate actual expenses (rent/mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, repairs) based on percentage of home used for business

Retirement Accounts for the Self-Employed

Contributing to retirement accounts is one of the best ways to reduce both income tax and self-employment tax while building wealth.

Solo 401(k)

The most powerful option for self-employed individuals with no employees:

  • Employee contribution: Up to $23,500 (2026)
  • Employer contribution: Up to 25% of compensation
  • Total max: $70,000 (or $77,500 with catch-up for 50+)
  • Can include Roth option

SEP IRA

Simple to set up and administer:

  • Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment earnings (after SE tax deduction)
  • Maximum: $70,000 (2026)
  • Traditional (tax-deductible) contributions only

SIMPLE IRA

Good for small businesses with employees:

  • Employee contribution: Up to $16,500
  • Employer match or non-elective contribution required
  • Lower limits than Solo 401(k) or SEP
üí° Solo 401(k) vs. SEP IRA: For most self-employed individuals without employees, a Solo 401(k) allows higher contributions at lower income levels. SEP IRAs are simpler but only allow employer contributions (25% of compensation).

Choosing the Right Business Structure

Your business structure affects how much self-employment tax you pay:

Sole Proprietorship / Single-Member LLC

  • All net income subject to SE tax
  • Simplest structure, lowest compliance costs
  • Best for lower-income businesses

S-Corporation Election

  • Pay yourself a "reasonable salary" ‚Üí subject to payroll tax
  • Remaining profits pass through as distributions ‚Üí NO SE tax
  • Can save significant taxes at higher income levels
  • More compliance requirements (payroll, separate tax return)

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I have to pay self-employment tax if I already have a full-time W-2 job?

Yes, if your net self-employment income is $400 or more. However, you may hit the Social Security wage base cap ($176,100) from your combined income, which would reduce the SE tax on your side income.

Can I deduct business expenses even if I don't itemize?

Yes! Business expenses on Schedule C are separate from itemized deductions. You can take the standard deduction AND deduct business expenses.

What if I can't afford to pay quarterly estimated taxes?

You'll owe interest and penalties on underpaid taxes. Set aside 25-30% of each payment you receive for taxes to avoid cash flow problems. Consider opening a separate savings account for taxes.

How do I know if I should elect S-Corp status?

Generally, S-Corp election makes sense when net business income exceeds roughly $50,000-$80,000 AND the tax savings exceed the additional compliance costs ($1,000-$2,000/year for accountant, payroll service, etc.). Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

Are LLC members subject to self-employment tax?

Single-member LLCs are treated as sole proprietorships for tax purposes ‚Äî yes, SE tax applies. Multi-member LLCs (partnerships) may have more complex rules. LLCs that elect S-Corp or C-Corp taxation follow different rules.

What happens if I don't pay self-employment tax?

You'll face penalties and interest on unpaid amounts. Serious non-compliance can lead to IRS collections actions, wage garnishment, or liens.

Summary

Self-employment tax is a significant consideration for freelancers, contractors, and small business owners. Key takeaways:

  • SE tax rate is 15.3% (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) on 92.35% of net earnings
  • Make quarterly payments to avoid underpayment penalties (Apr 15, Jun 15, Sep 15, Jan 15)
  • Deduct business expenses aggressively but legitimately to reduce your tax base
  • Consider S-Corp election at higher income levels to reduce SE tax
  • Max out retirement accounts (Solo 401(k), SEP IRA) to reduce taxable income
  • Set aside 25-30% of income for taxes throughout the year

Planning ahead and understanding your obligations is the key to managing self-employment taxes without stress. Consider working with a tax professional who specializes in self-employment to maximize your deductions and ensure compliance.
